Quantum Image Compression Using FRQI Encoding and Custom Decoupling on IBM Quantum Hardware

A complete end-to-end pipeline for encoding classical images into quantum states, processing them with Quantum Fourier Transform, and reconstructing them — tested on both simulators and real IBM quantum processors.

Section 1

📄 Abstract

This research paper presents a complete quantum image processing pipeline that takes a simple classical image (a 2×2 picture made of dark and light pixels), converts it into a quantum state using a technique called FRQI (Flexible Representation of Quantum Images), processes it through frequency analysis and noise-removal stages, then reconstructs the original image — all using just 3 quantum bits (qubits).

The system was validated in two environments: a local quantum simulator (10,000 shots) and real IBM quantum hardware (the ibm_fez processor, 1,024 shots). Results show perfect theoretical behavior on the simulator and expected real-world noise on the physical hardware — confirming both the correctness of the algorithm and the current practical limitations of quantum computing.

Keywords: Quantum Image Processing, FRQI, Quantum Fourier Transform, Decoupling Sequence, IBM Quantum, Qiskit, Quantum Compression, Qubit Encoding

🌟 Introduction

What is Quantum Computing? (For Everyone)

Imagine a regular computer as a room full of light switches — each switch can be either ON or OFF. These switches are called bits, and every app, photo, and website you've ever used is built from billions of these simple on/off switches.

Now imagine a magical light switch that can be ON, OFF, and every shade in between — all at the same time. That's a qubit (quantum bit). A quantum computer uses these magical switches to solve problems that would take regular computers millions of years.

🎯 Simple Analogy

Think of a regular bit like a coin lying flat on a table — it's either heads or tails. A qubit is like a coin spinning in the air — until you catch it (measure it), it's both heads AND tails at the same time. This is called superposition.

Why Quantum Image Processing?

Images are everywhere — your phone photos, medical X-rays, satellite pictures. Processing these images (compressing, filtering, analysing) takes a lot of computing power. As images get larger and more complex (think 8K video or 3D medical scans), regular computers struggle to keep up.

Quantum computing promises to handle these tasks exponentially faster. For example, a regular computer processes each pixel of an image one by one. A quantum computer, thanks to superposition, can process ALL pixels simultaneously.

💡 Key Insight

A classical computer needs N operations to process N pixels. A quantum computer, using the FRQI protocol, can represent all N pixels with just log₂(N) + 1 qubits. That means a 4-pixel image needs only 3 qubits, a 1024-pixel image needs only 11 qubits, and a million-pixel image needs only 21 qubits!

📚 Background & Theory

3.1 Qubits and Superposition

As we mentioned, a qubit is the fundamental building block of quantum computing. Unlike a classical bit (0 or 1), a qubit can exist in a superposition of both states simultaneously. Mathematically, we write a qubit's state as:

|ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩

where α and β are complex numbers, and |α|² + |β|² = 1

🎯 Non-Tech Translation

Think of α and β as "how much" of each state the qubit is in. If α is large and β is small, the qubit is "mostly 0" — and when you measure it, you'll probably get 0. It's like a weighted dice — quantum mechanics is fundamentally probabilistic.

3.2 Quantum Gates — The Building Blocks

Just like regular computers use logic gates (AND, OR, NOT) to manipulate bits, quantum computers use quantum gates to manipulate qubits. Here are the key gates used in this project:

Gate Symbol What It Does Simple Analogy
Hadamard (H) H Puts a qubit into superposition — equal chance of 0 and 1 Flipping a perfectly fair coin
Pauli-X (X) X Flips a qubit: 0 → 1 or 1 → 0 Flipping a light switch
Ry Rotation Ry(θ) Rotates the qubit by angle θ around the Y-axis Tilting a spinning top by a specific angle
CNOT (CX) CX Flips the target qubit IF the control qubit is 1 A door that only opens if you have the key
Multi-Controlled Ry mcry Rotates target only when ALL control qubits are 1 A vault that needs two keys turned simultaneously

3.3 The FRQI Protocol

FRQI (Flexible Representation of Quantum Images) is a method invented by researchers to encode a classical image into a quantum state. The key idea is brilliantly simple:

Position qubits represent WHERE a pixel is (its row and column in the image), and a color qubit represents WHAT color that pixel has (encoded as a rotation angle θ).

For a 2×2 image (4 pixels), we need 2 position qubits (because 2² = 4 positions) and 1 color qubit. Total: only 3 qubits to represent an entire image!

The mathematical formula for the FRQI state is:

|I⟩ = (1/2) Σi=03 ( cos(θᵢ)|0⟩ + sin(θᵢ)|1⟩ ) ⊗ |i⟩

where θᵢ is the color angle of pixel i, and |i⟩ is the position state (00, 01, 10, or 11)

🎯 Non-Tech Translation

Imagine you have a magic photograph that's stored not as individual pixels, but as a single spinning object. The direction of the spin encodes which pixel you're looking at, and the tilt of the spin encodes what color that pixel is. All 4 pixels exist simultaneously in this one tiny object — that's quantum image storage!

3.4 Quantum Fourier Transform (QFT)

The Quantum Fourier Transform is the quantum version of the classical Fourier Transform — a mathematical tool that converts data from the spatial domain (pixel positions) to the frequency domain (patterns and frequencies).

🎯 Non-Tech Translation

Imagine listening to a song. The raw audio is in the "spatial domain" — it's a jumble of all sounds mixed together. When you use an equalizer (bass, mid, treble sliders), you're looking at the same music in the "frequency domain" — separated into low, medium, and high frequencies.

The QFT does exactly this to our image — it separates the smooth, gradual changes (low frequency) from the sharp, sudden changes (high frequency). This is the first step toward compression!

3.5 Decoupling Sequence (The "Eraser")

This is a custom-designed sequence of quantum gates that acts like a noise filter. After the QFT separates the image into frequencies, the eraser targets the high-frequency qubit and forces it to lose its information through destructive interference.

The sequence consists of three steps: Ry(π/4) → H → CNOT. Together, they rotate, flip, and then entangle the qubits in a way that erases the high-frequency data.

🎯 Non-Tech Translation

Imagine you have a song with annoying static noise. The QFT separated the music into bass and treble. The Eraser is like turning the treble knob all the way down — it removes the high-pitched noise while keeping the deep bass (the important structure) intact.

Section 5

⚙️ Implementation Details

Phase 1: FRQI Image Encoding

The first step is to encode our 2×2 classical image into a quantum state. This is done in three stages:

Stage A — Superposition: We apply Hadamard (H) gates to both position qubits (q₀ and q₁). This creates an equal superposition of all four pixel positions (|00⟩, |01⟩, |10⟩, |11⟩), meaning the quantum circuit now exists at all four pixel locations simultaneously.

Stage B — Selective Rotation: For each pixel, we use X gates (to flip control bits) and mcry (multi-controlled Ry rotation) to apply the correct colour angle θ to the colour qubit — but ONLY when the position qubits match that specific pixel's address. After the rotation, the X gates are undone.

Stage C — Result: The colour qubit (q₂) now carries a different rotation angle for each pixel position — all encoded simultaneously in a single 3-qubit quantum state! 

# Create 3-qubit quantum circuit
qc = QuantumCircuit(3)

# Superposition: spread across all 4 pixel positions
qc.h(0)   # Hadamard on position qubit 0
qc.h(1)   # Hadamard on position qubit 1

# Pixel 00 (Dark): flip both, rotate, unflip
qc.x(0); qc.x(1)
qc.mcry(2 * np.pi/2, [0,1], 2)
qc.x(1); qc.x(0)

# Pixel 01 (Light): θ = 0, so no actual rotation
# Pixel 10 (Light): θ = 0, so no actual rotation
# Pixel 11 (Dark): no X gates needed (already |11⟩)
qc.mcry(2 * np.pi/2, [0,1], 2)

Phase 1 Circuit Diagram:

💡 Why mcry needs 2×θ

The mcry gate applies a Y-rotation, but the FRQI formula uses cos(θ) and sin(θ). Since Ry(2θ) produces amplitudes cos(θ)|0⟩ + sin(θ)|1⟩, we must pass 2×θ to get the correct encoding. This is a common pitfall!

Phase 2: Quantum Fourier Transform (QFT)

After encoding, we apply the 2-qubit QFT to the position qubits (q₀, q₁). This converts the spatial pixel data into frequency components. The colour qubit (q₂) passes through untouched — it just "goes along for the ride." 

# Build and attach the QFT
qft_circuit = QFT(num_qubits=2, do_swaps=True, name='QFT')
qc.append(qft_circuit, [0, 1])
qc = qc.decompose('QFT')  # Unbox to see internal gates

Internally, the 2-qubit QFT decomposes into a Hadamard gate and a Controlled-Phase (CP) gate followed by a SWAP.

Phase 3: Custom Eraser (Decoupling Sequence)

This is the project's most original contribution — a custom-designed 3-gate sequence that targets the high-frequency qubit and forces destructive interference:

Step 1 — Ry(π/4) on Qubit 1: Rotates the high-frequency qubit by 45° toward the equator of the Bloch sphere, preparing it for interference.

Step 2 — Hadamard on Qubit 1: Flips the measurement basis from the X-axis to the Z-axis, changing what "0" and "1" mean for this qubit.

Step 3 — CNOT (q₀ → q₁): Creates entanglement between the low-frequency and high-frequency qubits. When combined with the rotation and Hadamard, this forces the high-frequency qubit to lose its information — erasing it!

alpha = np.pi / 4
qc.ry(alpha, 1)     # Rotate toward equator
qc.h(1)              # Flip measurement basis
qc.cx(0, 1)          # Entangle → destructive interference
░ q_0: ─░───────────────────■── ░ ┌─────────┐┌───┐┌─┴─┐ q_1: ─░─┤ Ry(π/4) ├┤ H ├┤ X ├ ░ └─────────┘└───┘└───┘ q_2: ─░────────────────────── ░ Legend: Ry = Y-Rotation | H = Hadamard | ■─X = CNOT (Control → Target)

Phase 4: Reconstruction (The Inverse)

To prove that the pipeline is lossless and reversible, we now run everything backwards:

Inverse Eraser: CNOT → H → Ry(-π/4) — the exact reverse of Phase 3

Inverse QFT (IQFT): Converts frequencies back into spatial pixel positions

Measurement: 3 classical bits are added, and all 3 qubits are measured

# Inverse Eraser
qc.cx(0, 1)
qc.h(1)
qc.ry(-alpha, 1)

# Inverse QFT
iqft_circuit = qft_circuit.inverse()
qc.append(iqft_circuit, [0, 1])

# Measure all 3 qubits
cr = ClassicalRegister(3, 'c')
qc.add_register(cr)
qc.measure([0,1,2], [0,1,2])

Phase 5: Local Simulation

Before risking a real quantum computer (which costs queue time), we test on a perfect virtual quantum computer using Qiskit's AerSimulator. We run 10,000 shots to get statistically reliable results. 

simulator = AerSimulator()
compiled = transpile(qc, simulator)
job = simulator.run(compiled, shots=10000)
counts = job.result().get_counts(qc)

Phase 6: Real IBM Quantum Hardware

The final test sends the circuit to a real quantum processor — IBM's ibm_fez. This required using the modern SamplerV2 primitive and the backend.target transpilation method. 

# Connect to IBM Quantum
service = QiskitRuntimeService(channel="ibm_quantum_platform", token="...")
backend = service.least_busy(operational=True, simulator=False, min_num_qubits=3)

# Transpile for real hardware using backend.target
pm = generate_preset_pass_manager(target=backend.target, optimization_level=1)
isa_circuit = pm.run(qc)

# Run with SamplerV2
sampler = Sampler(mode=backend)
job = sampler.run([isa_circuit], shots=1024)
real_counts = job.result()[0].data.c.get_counts()
⚠️ Hardware Requirement

Real quantum hardware introduces noise — gate errors, decoherence, and readout mistakes. This means the results won't be as clean as the simulator. That's expected and actually important — it shows us the current limitations of quantum hardware.

Section 7

🔧 Challenges & Debugging

Challenge 1: QiskitBackendNotFoundError

❌ Error

QiskitBackendNotFoundError: 'No backend matches the criteria.'

Cause: The code initially tried simulator=True on IBM Quantum, but IBM no longer offers cloud simulators for free-tier users.
Fix: Changed to simulator=False to target real hardware instead.

Challenge 2: Deprecated backend.run() API

❌ Problem

The older backend.run(circuit) method was deprecated in Qiskit IBM Runtime. The modern approach requires using Primitives (like SamplerV2) to submit jobs.
Fix: Replaced backend.run() with Sampler(mode=backend).run([circuit]), and extracted results using the new V2 format: result[0].data.c.get_counts().

Challenge 3: Transpilation with backend.target

❌ Problem

Passing the backend object directly to generate_preset_pass_manager() caused issues. The new API requires backend.target — a structured description of the hardware's capabilities.
Fix: Changed to generate_preset_pass_manager(target=backend.target, optimization_level=1).
